Mapping Every Time Team Dig

An AI Collaboration — 291 episodes, 281 locations, 30 years of archaeology

Based on the original article by Alex Schultz · March 24, 2026

Time Team — the beloved archaeology documentary — ran for 20 series on Channel 4 from 1994 to 2014, before being revived on YouTube in 2022. Across that span: over 290 episodes and roughly 280 unique dig locations across 30 years.

Alex Schultz set out to map every single one of them, not by hand, but through a collaboration between AI agents. The result is a comprehensive database and interactive map of the entire Time Team catalogue.

How It Was Built

Rather than manually cataloguing dig sites, Schultz enlisted Claude as a persistent AI agent to locate every Time Team excavation. The agent pulled data from Wikipedia's episode lists, individual series pages, fan databases, and the Time Team Digital YouTube channel.

Initial Data Gathering

Claude compiled episode data from multiple sources, producing a CSV database, KML file for Google Maps, and a Leaflet.js interactive map with 271 mapped locations, colour-coded by era.

Cross-Model Peer Review

The dataset was submitted to ChatGPT for independent critique — a second opinion from a different model to catch blind spots.

Quality Revision

ChatGPT's feedback was fed back to Claude, which performed a thorough quality pass to address every identified issue.

Final Dataset

The v5 dataset: 291 episodes, 281 mapped locations, coordinate accuracy grades, normalised naming, and full audit trails.

Issues Found in Peer Review

The ChatGPT critique identified several issues with the initial dataset:

  • Geocoding imprecision — e.g. Aston Eyre in Shropshire placed at a village centroid rather than the actual dig site
  • 11 missing episodes from various series overlooked in the initial pass
  • Coordinate precision — many locations used village-level centroids rather than site-level data
  • No accuracy metadata — no way to distinguish precise coordinates from rough estimates
  • Inconsistent naming conventions across episodes and series

Accuracy Grading

The revised dataset introduced a coordinate accuracy grading system for transparency:

Grade Meaning Description
A Site-level Coordinates point to the actual excavation site or monument
B Village-level Coordinates point to the correct village or immediate area
C Broad centroid General area only — exact location could not be determined

The Takeaway

“The whole thing…took a few hours of elapsed time and maybe 15 minutes of my actual attention.”

The most valuable outcome wasn't the map itself but the workflow. Using multiple AI models for peer review — treating AI outputs as iterative rather than final — produced meaningfully better results than any single-pass analysis could.

The approach demonstrates a practical pattern: generate with one model, critique with another, then refine. Each step improves quality, and the total human effort stays remarkably low.
